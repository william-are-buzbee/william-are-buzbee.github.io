# Planetary Ecology Foundations

Reference document for creature design, biome logic, and world-building. These are upstream physical facts about the planet — they constrain everything downstream. Include this alongside Lore.md and Spawn-Design.md when designing creatures, biomes, or environments.

---

## The Six Parameters

### 1. Star and Orbit

**Intensity:** ~60-70% of Earth. The world feels like perpetual late afternoon — warm-toned, dim, soft shadows. Not dark, but no harsh midday glare. Night is genuinely dark due to low ambient light.

**Spectrum:** Yellow-orange peak (slightly cooler than Sol). Photosynthetic organisms evolve pigments that absorb blue, green, and violet, reflecting reds and deep browns. The raw photosynthetic pigment is deep red-violet; under the amber starlight, this shifts to the deep crimson and dark red-brown the player sees. Trunks are not photosynthetic — they're mineral-impregnated structural material tinted by local chemistry (iron-red, copper-green, manganese-dark). Chemotrophic organisms (which don't photosynthesize) may actually be *lighter* in color than photosynthetic ones — their pigmentation comes from mineral chemistry, not light absorption. See the Color Interpretation Guide section for the three-layer color model (material × starlight × creature perception) and specific hex values.

**Consistency:** Very steady. The star barely fluctuates. No boom-bust energy cycles. Evolution favors efficiency over resilience: slow metabolisms, long lifespans, complex symbioses, minimal waste. Sudden disruptions (like the demigod's wipes) are catastrophic precisely because nothing evolved to handle them.

### 2. Planetary Mass and Geology

**Gravity:** Near-Earth (~0.9-1.1g). Familiar body plans. No special justification needed for walking, swimming, climbing, or fighting. The planet has enough mass to retain a thick atmosphere.

**Geological activity:** Highly active. Volcanism, mineral cycling, and geothermal energy are widespread. The planet continuously pumps fresh minerals to the surface through hot springs, seeps, and erosion of new rock. Underground ecosystems are self-sustaining — they run on geothermal energy, not filtered sunlight. The deeper you go, the more the ecology runs on planetary chemistry.

**Crustal chemistry:** Mixed metallic — copper, iron, and manganese are all present in significant concentrations, distributed unevenly across the crust. Different regions have different dominant minerals, producing different local chemistries, different organism metabolisms, and different color palettes. This is the primary driver of biome identity.

Regional chemistry examples:
- **Copper-rich zones:** Blue-green mineral deposits, turquoise-tinted water, organisms with verdigris-green or blue structural compounds.
- **Iron-rich zones:** Rust-red soil, ochre-amber water, organisms with reddish-brown shells/bark/plating.
- **Manganese-rich zones:** Purple-black deposits, dark violet pigmentation. Chemotrophs here metabolize manganese compounds.
- **Mineral-depleted zones:** Pale, dry, sparse. The geological equivalent of a desert — not hot and arid, but chemically dead. Rare, notable when they appear.

### 3. Atmosphere

**Density:** Slightly thicker than Earth (~1.1-1.3 atm). Retains heat and moisture effectively. Temperature swings are mild. Combined with the dim star, produces permanently soft, diffuse light. Sound carries further and more clearly than on Earth — hearing-based detection is effective.

**Moisture:** Moderate humidity with seasonal mist. Wet regions are very wet; dry regions exist but are rare and geologically unusual (mineral-depleted areas where the water table dropped). Fog and mist roll through wet biomes periodically.

**Breathing gas:** Comparable to Earth's oxygen (~19-23%). Familiar energy levels. Fire behaves normally. No metabolic weirdness for the player or creatures.

### 4. Hydrosphere

**Mineral concentration:** Moderate in surface water (noticeably mineral-rich but still translucent). Organisms need specialized structures to concentrate minerals — armor and hard structures are *possible* but *costly*. Some lineages invest heavily in mineral plating; many remain soft-bodied. Armor is a strategy, not a default.

Underground water is much richer — percolating through hot, chemically active rock concentrates minerals. Underground aquatic biomes are more hospitable than surface water for mineral-dependent organisms.

**Temperature:** Cool-to-warm gradient. Most surface water is temperate. High elevation or polar regions can freeze — ice exists but is rare and locally significant. Geothermal activity keeps underground water warm. Deep aquatic environments are warm and mineral-dense.

Ice is alien on this planet. Most organisms have never encountered it. Creatures adapted to frozen environments are rare specialists occupying a genuinely unusual microenvironment that most life can't survive in.

**pH:** Near neutral (~6.5-7.5). Both calcium-based and metallic biology work. Some lineages build with bone-like calcium structures (pale, familiar). Others build with iron or copper compounds (dark, metallic, heavier). Both are valid evolutionary strategies. Visual variety is a chemical consequence, not an aesthetic choice.

### 5. Wipe Survivors — The Two Clades

The demigod's failed experiments in ecosystem-building ended in wipes — mass destruction of the surface ecology. Two organisms survived. Whether they were intentionally preserved, accidentally overlooked, or simply in niches the wipe couldn't reach is unclear and may be unknowable. What matters is: these two organisms are the common ancestors of all complex life on the planet. Every creature the player encounters descends from one or the other.

They were both designed by the same demigod, so they share deep structural assumptions — similar cell biology, similar basic metabolic pathways, similar "building blocks." The differences between them are real but more like two variations on a theme than two unrelated phyla from different planets. Two prototypes from the same workshop. Different enough that evolution took them in very different directions. Similar enough that they compete for the same niches.

**Complexity at time of the wipe:** Roughly Cambrian-level. Multicellular, with established concepts of predation, passive feeding, armor, locomotion, and basic sensory organs. Body plans are functional but experimental — the roles (predator, grazer, sessile organism) exist, but the solutions are weird, unoptimized, and varied. Given enough time and mutation, both organisms radiated into every available niche on the planet.

**Neither clade "owns" a niche.** Both clades independently evolved chemotrophs and phototroph-symbiotes, predators and prey, aquatic and terrestrial forms, motile strategies of every kind. A chemotrophic niche like a mineral seep has animals from *both* clades exploiting it — but using different body plans, different metabolisms, different strategies. The same is true of every animal niche. When two creatures fill the same role (same biome, same diet, same size), they may be from different clades, which means they arrived at the same solution from different starting hardware. Convergence is common; shared ancestry within a niche is not guaranteed. The one niche neither animal clade fills is primary producer — that belongs to the flora, an entirely separate lineage (see Flora section below).

#### Clade A — The Ancestor

**Size at time of the wipe:** Roughly dog-sized. Lower to the ground, broad body.

**Body plan:** Three pairs of limbs (six total). The front pair is shorter, more dexterous, almost manipulative. The remaining pairs are locomotory. The body is segmented but not in an arthropod way — distinct flexible sections covered in thick, textured skin (wrinkled, tough, like the skin on an elephant's trunk or a naked mole rat). Not fur, not shell, not chitin. Something else.

**Sensory layout:** Two primary eyes set wide, almost lateral. A cluster of three smaller eyes low and forward, near the ventral mouth, for close-range tracking. The mouth is on the underside, not the front. Mobile ear-like flaps on top of the head serve as the primary "readable" feature — they fold, splay, and rotate. They communicate emotional and attentional state and are the closest thing to a face the player can interpret intuitively.

**Primary senses:** Chemical sensing (olfaction, surface chemoreception) is dominant. Vision is secondary — used for close-range detail and threat detection, not navigation or world-modeling. A Clade A descendant's internal world-model is a chemical/scent map layered with emotional associations, not a visual scene.

**Cognition — centralized, episodic:** One brain. Strong episodic memory — it remembers *events*, builds a personal history, and changes behavior based on accumulated experience. Individuals get meaningfully smarter with age. They recognize other individuals, develop idiosyncratic habits based on what has happened to them personally. Generalization and abstract reasoning are strong. They can apply lessons learned in one context to novel situations.

Practical behavioral consequences:
- Commits to a behavioral state (hunting, fleeing, patrolling, resting) and transitions between states cleanly. There is a readable evaluation moment when conditions change.
- Tracks prey or threats over long distances via chemical trail. Patient, persistent.
- Can be visually sneaked past more easily than chemically evaded.
- Experienced individuals are more dangerous than young ones — not because of size, but learned behavior.
- Social groups have clear hierarchies with distinct roles. Individuals are recognizable and behaviorally distinct.

**AI pattern:** State machine with clear transitions. One active behavior at a time. Predictable once you learn the triggers, but the triggers may involve the creature's personal history (prior encounters, territory familiarity, social rank).

**Reproduction — sequential hermaphroditism:** An individual is born as one sex and can change to the other under specific social conditions. The smaller/subordinate individual in a mating pair becomes the gestating parent. At any given moment, each organism is fully one sex — but it isn't locked there permanently. Any two adults can reproduce; social dynamics determine who carries offspring.

Downstream consequences:
- Social structures revolve around dominance hierarchies where reproductive roles are at stake.
- Size and territory matter enormously — dominant individuals maintain their non-gestating sex.
- Populations are genetically diverse at all times (sexual reproduction is the default).
- Speciation is gradual — Clade A branches slowly and steadily, like a tree.

#### Clade B — The Ancestor

**Size at time of the wipe:** Prawn-sized. Stocky for its size, not delicate. Small enough to hold in a hand, but dense.

**Body plan:** Four pairs of limbs (eight total). The front two pairs are longer, thinner, covered in chemical-sensing structures. The rear two pairs are robust and locomotory. The body can curl defensively and flatten to squeeze through gaps. The integument is semi-flexible layered material — harder than Clade A's skin but not rigid shell. Something like cuttlefish bone crossed with fingernail. Can stiffen or soften. Over evolutionary time, some descendants evolved this into full armor; others thinned it to near-skin.

**Sensory layout:** Four eyes arranged in a diamond pattern at the front — two larger on top, two smaller below. Dark and reflective. Below the eyes, complex mouthparts that look busy and intricate. The overall impression is attentive and unreadable. The creature is clearly watching but gives little away about its internal state.

**Primary senses:** Vibration and close-range vision. The eyes are excellent at detecting motion and pattern but weak at distance. The body surface, especially the front limb pairs, is densely covered in mechanoreceptors — it reads the ground and the air for vibration, pressure changes, and chemical traces by touch. A Clade B descendant's internal world-model is a tactile/vibrational map, not a visual or olfactory one. It is accurate within the territory it knows through direct physical experience, and significantly less capable in unfamiliar ground.

**Cognition — distributed, pattern-matched:** Not one brain. Several ganglion clusters distributed through the body segments, each processing local sensory input and generating behavioral impulses. Behavior emerges from consensus across the clusters. When all ganglia agree, the organism acts instantly and decisively. The architecture supports parallel processing — different body regions can execute different motor programs simultaneously. The front limbs can be investigating a crevice while the rear limbs reposition the body, managed by different ganglion clusters without top-down coordination.

This architecture does NOT produce visible indecision or self-contradiction — evolution stamped that out. It produces something subtler: an organism that is always doing several things at once, that doesn't have a single "state" the way Clade A does, and that reacts to local stimuli with extremely fast trained responses but struggles with genuinely novel situations.

Strong *pattern* memory (stimulus-response associations trained locally in each ganglion), weak *episodic* memory (no unified narrator stitching events into a personal history). Incredible within a familiar environment. Measurably less effective in unfamiliar territory — not confused, but lacking the locally-trained response library.

**The crucial scaling property:** The ganglion clusters communicate via chemical signaling. When multiple Clade B organisms are near each other, the chemical signals *between* individuals become indistinguishable from the signals *within* an individual. Nearby organisms begin to partially synchronize — not by choice, but because the neural architecture can't distinguish internal from external signals at close range. This is a structural property of the nervous system, not a social behavior.

Evolution ran with this in different directions:
- **Solitary lineages** evolved to suppress the cross-talk. Fully individual, no synchronization. Behaviorally varied, genetically diverse.
- **Colonial lineages** leaned into it. Multiple organisms synchronizing into a distributed superorganism. The "colony" thinks as one entity using the same ganglion-consensus architecture that a single organism uses internally, just at a larger scale. Individual nodes are expendable. The colony is the organism.
- **Everything in between.** Semi-social clusters that partially synchronize when close. Family groups that blur the line between cooperation and merger. The spectrum from "individual" to "colony" is continuous in Clade B, not a binary.

Practical behavioral consequences:
- Can attack and reposition simultaneously — different body segments operate in parallel.
- Does not have readable behavioral states. There's no evaluation moment between states. Multiple behaviors are always running concurrently, weighted by urgency.
- Lethal within familiar territory, measurably less effective outside it.
- Solitary individuals are unpredictable — fast reactions, no consistent personality, different ganglia may dominate in different moments.
- Groups of nearby individuals, especially genetically similar ones (clones from recent fragmentation), will semi-synchronize. More individuals nearby = more coordinated = more dangerous. The coordination is not tactical — it's neurological.

**AI pattern:** Multiple concurrent behavior layers weighted by urgency, running in parallel. No single active state. The player cannot "read" a Clade B creature the way they read Clade A. Instead, the player manages them spatially — keeping individuals separated so they don't sync, staying out of their home territory, not allowing encirclement.

**Reproduction — simultaneous hermaphroditism + fragmentation:** Every adult is both sexes at all times. Can give and receive reproductive material in the same encounter. Additionally, can reproduce asexually through fragmentation — budding off a smaller, genetically identical copy of itself.

The dual strategy creates a distinctive population pattern:
- When conditions are stable, Clade B organisms fragment — cloning themselves, filling a niche rapidly with genetically identical individuals. Colonial behavior works well because identical neural architecture synchronizes cleanly.
- When conditions shift, sexual reproduction kicks in — producing genetically novel offspring, increasing diversity, enabling adaptation.
- Populations oscillate between genetic uniformity (cloning in good times) and bursts of diversity (sex in bad times).

Downstream consequences:
- Speciation happens in sudden bursts after environmental disruption, then goes quiet. Clade B's evolutionary tree is punctuated, not gradual.
- Colonial superorganisms are typically made of clones — which is why synchronization works so well. A colony of genetically diverse members would synchronize poorly.
- Solitary Clade B lineages that lean on sexual reproduction are the most genetically varied, individually distinct creatures on the planet.

#### How the Two Clades Coexist in Shared Niches

Both clades radiated into every available niche. No niche belongs to one clade. In any given biome, the player encounters descendants of both — filling similar roles through different means.

**Same niche, different solutions:** Two predators in the same forest. The Clade A descendant hunts by chemical trail, commits to a chase, has a personality the player can learn to predict. The Clade B descendant hunts by vibration and ground-knowledge within its home range, runs multiple behavioral threads simultaneously, and can't be "read" the same way. Both are dangerous. Both are learnable. But the skills that work against one don't transfer cleanly to the other.

**Chemotrophic niches:** Both clades evolved chemotrophs independently. Clade A's chemotrophic descendants tend toward solitary, territorial, memory-driven resource exploitation — they remember where the good deposits are and defend them. Clade B's chemotrophic descendants range from solitary mineral-processors to vast colonial networks that slowly consume an entire mineral field as a distributed organism. Underground territories may have both types coexisting uneasily.

**Aquatic niches:** Both clades went aquatic. Clade A descendants tend toward larger, migratory, territorial marine life with individual memory and personality. Clade B descendants fill every size category — from colonial filter-feeders to solitary swimming predators to organisms that are functionally reef structures.

**The player's experience:** In any biome, creatures from both clades are present. The player gradually learns that there are two fundamentally different kinds of organism in the world — not by being told, but by noticing that some creatures have readable states, chemical-tracking behavior, and individual personality, while others are parallel-processing, territory-bound, and synchronization-prone. The two clades feel different to fight, different to avoid, and different to observe.

### Flora — The Third Lineage

The two animal clades are not the only life on the planet. The demigod's wipes destroyed complex ecosystems but could not sterilize the microbial base layer — bacterial mats, simple algal films, chemotrophic microorganisms embedded in soil, rock, and water. These were too small, too widespread, and too resilient to be affected by surface-level destruction. They persisted through every failed experiment, completely beneath the demigod's attention.

This microbial base layer is the ancestor of all flora on the planet. The "trees" and "grasses" and "fungal mats" are not descendants of either animal clade. They are the scaled-up descendants of microorganisms that were already photosynthesizing and processing minerals long before the two animal survivors began radiating. Animals don't become trees for the same reason a bear doesn't become a gas station — the gas station was already there, doing gas station things far more efficiently than any animal could pivot to.

Flora splits along the same energy lines that the ancestral microbes already occupied:

#### Photosynthetic Flora

Descended from photosynthetic microbial mats. Scaled up into large sessile organisms the same way Earth algae scaled up into land plants — gradually evolving structural support, water transport, and reproductive strategies for terrestrial life.

**Form factor — fern-forest, not modern forest.** The flora has reached roughly fern-to-early-tree complexity. No flowers (flowers coevolved with insect pollinators on Earth; this planet's animal clades haven't produced the fine-motor specialized pollinators to drive that). No fruit. No seeds. Reproduction is through spores — released as visible clouds from structures on frond undersides, wind-dispersed in the thick atmosphere (thick air carries spores further and slower, keeping them airborne longer). A "blooming" event on this planet is a dark forest releasing a spore haze that drifts through the understory.

**Growth habit — broad and low, not tall.** Earth trees grow tall because direct sunlight creates intense vertical competition — being one meter higher than your neighbor shades them out. This planet's dim star and thick atmosphere produce diffuse light from all angles. The advantage of height is reduced. The advantage of being *broad* — maximizing surface area to catch light from every direction — is increased. The dominant photosynthetic flora is structurally between a tree fern and a Carboniferous club moss: short trunks (3-8 meters, not 30), broad spreading canopies of fractal fronds radiating outward. Walking through a forest feels more like walking through a room with a dark, low ceiling than standing among tall columns with a distant canopy overhead.

**Color — the photosynthetic anchor.**

The color of living photosynthetic tissue is the visual anchor of this world. Understanding it requires separating three layers:

1. **Material color (color in a vacuum):** The photosynthetic pigments absorb blue, green, and violet aggressively under any light. The material itself — the actual tissue — is deep red-violet. This is the base pigment. In neutral white light, a frond would look deep crimson-maroon. There is no green anywhere in the photosynthetic flora. The trunks are separate — built from mineral-impregnated structural material (dense, ceramic-like, not woody), colored by whatever minerals are locally incorporated: iron-red, copper-green, manganese-dark. Trunk color varies by region; frond/mat color does not.

2. **Environmental modification (what light does to it):** The yellow-orange star warms and dims everything. The deep red-violet pigment, lit by amber starlight, shifts darker and browner. Under full open-sky illumination, living photosynthetic surface appears deep red-brown — still visibly red, but warmer and darker than the raw pigment. Under forest canopy shade (low ceiling, 3-8m), the same material darkens further to near-black. The amount of light reaching the surface is the primary driver of apparent brightness. A frond catching open sky looks deep crimson. The same frond under canopy shadow looks almost black.

3. **Perceptual interpretation (what the animal sees):** The creatures on this planet evolved under this dim star. Their visual transducers compensate by amplifying the signal — brightening the visual field to extract usable contrast from a dark world. What the player sees on screen is this post-perceptual version: the creature's visual system has brightened a near-black world into something navigable. The raw reflected light is much darker than what's rendered. The warm brown tones of the ground, the visible crimson of the trees — these are the creature's interpretation, not the "true" color of the surface under raw starlight.

**In-game reference colors (what the player's creature perceives):**
- Living photosynthetic surface (fronds, tree canopy): deep crimson-red. Game hex: forest fg `#6a2c24`.
- Forest floor under canopy: near-black with red undertone. Game hex: forest bg `#2e160e`.
- Open-ground photosynthetic mats (grassland): warm red-brown, noticeably lighter than forest floor. Game hex: plains fg `#7a4c38` on bg `#342818`.

**Ground cover** is mat-forming: low, continuous photosynthetic surfaces carpeting the ground. Not individual grass blades — continuous mats, like moss scaled up. But the ground is NOT uniform red, and it is NOT the same color as the living canopy above it. The ground surface is a mix of:

- **Living photosynthetic mat** — the red-spectrum anchor. Darkest and most red where actively growing in shade.
- **Dead and decomposing mat material** — when the photosynthetic pigment breaks down in dead tissue, the underlying structural chemistry shows through. Dead mat trends browner, warmer, and lighter than living mat. The organic detritus layer is amber-brown, not red.
- **Mineral substrate showing through** — the mat doesn't cover everything. Patches of exposed soil and mineral substrate break up the photosynthetic surface. Soil color depends on local chemistry (iron-rich = red-brown, copper-rich = greenish-brown, manganese = dark purple-brown).

The result: open grassland ground is a mix of living dark-red mat, dead amber-brown detritus, and mineral substrate, producing an overall warm brown tone with scattered reddish accent. It's visibly different from the deep crimson of the tree canopy. Forest floor is darker overall (shade) but has the same material mix at lower brightness.

**The "forest" biome is a dark, wet, low-ceilinged fern swamp.** Standing water between trunks, saturated soil, dark fronds overhead, spore haze in the dim amber light. More flooded mangrove bog than temperate woodland.

#### Chemotrophic Flora

Descended from chemotrophic microbial mats. Scaled up into the fungal-zone producers — mats, mounded structures, bracket shelving, fruiting bodies. They process minerals and don't need light.

**Form factor — stromatolite-descended.** The chemotrophic flora's growth forms are driven by maximizing contact with mineral-rich substrate rather than catching light. Three main structure types:

*Colony mounds* — the main body of the organism. Domed, layered structures rising from the ground, resembling scaled-up stromatolites. Built up over time in concentric mineral-encrusted layers. The mound IS the organism (or the visible above-ground portion of a colony network spreading below the surface).

*Bracket shelving* — horizontal plate-like structures projecting outward from exposed rock faces. Stacked vertically with gaps between them, each shelf a flat extension of the colony reaching out to process minerals directly from the stone. Growth layers visible as concentric lines on each shelf.

*Fruiting spires* — thin vertical stalks rising from colony mounds, capped with spore-release structures. These are reproductive organs, not separate organisms. They project upward to catch air currents for spore dispersal. The most "mushroom-like" silhouette the player sees, but they're not mushrooms — they're one organ of a much larger colonial organism below.

**Color — mineral-derived.** Chemotrophic flora takes on the color of whatever mineral substrate it processes. In manganese-rich zones: purple-dark to violet-black. In copper-rich zones: blue-green to turquoise. In iron-rich zones: rusty ochre to amber-brown. A change in flora color within a chemotrophic zone means the underlying mineral chemistry changed. Color IS chemistry.

**Texture — mineral and geological.** The surfaces are layered, crusty, mineralized. More like calcium buildup or geological deposit than anything obviously organic. Some of it looks almost like stone until you notice it's grown since you were last here.

**The "fungal" biome is an open, mounded mineral flat.** No canopy — the amber sky is visible overhead, light hitting the ground directly. The landscape is warm brown rock and soil with purple-dark (or blue-green, or rusty) colony structures growing on and out of it. Mounds rise from the ground, bracket shelves project from rock faces, thin fruiting spires reach upward. The ground between mounds is a continuous mat network — the colony spreading between its visible structures. Walking through a fungal zone, the player is walking *on top of* the organism.

#### Regional Mineral Chemistry — Surface and Underground Connected

The planet's active interior continuously pushes reduced minerals upward through the crust. These minerals enter the surface soil through groundwater percolation, but the path is indirect — groundwater moves laterally through aquifers, surface processes rework the soil, and erosion and deposition redistribute material. The surface soil chemistry at any given point is a mix of local bedrock weathering, transported sediment, deep percolation, and organic accumulation. It reflects local geology, but not as a simple vertical projection of what's below.

**Mineral zones** are regions of relatively uniform mineral chemistry in the soil and substrate. They're created by the underlying geology — a volcanic island has zones of different composition depending on what rock types are present, what minerals the geological activity is pushing upward, and how surface water distributes dissolved minerals.

**Zone scale:** Mineral zones vary in size the way real geology does — no single uniform scale. Some examples:

- A large basaltic region might span 200-500+ tiles across, setting the default iron-rich chemistry for an entire island flank.
- A copper sulfide deposit surfaces over maybe 40-150 tiles, creating a distinct chemical pocket within the larger iron-rich region.
- A hydrothermal vent or hot spring enriches the soil in a small intense patch, maybe 15-40 tiles across, with chemistry quite different from the surrounding area.
- A stream carrying mineral-rich water from a deep source creates a narrow corridor of enriched soil along its banks, perhaps 5-15 tiles wide but running for hundreds of tiles along the water's path.
- The transition between zones is gradual — 10-30 tiles of blending where soil chemistry shifts from one dominant mineral to another.

The variation itself matters. A single island might have 3-6 major mineral zones of different sizes, with smaller pockets and corridors within them. The landscape is a patchwork, not a grid. Some zones are large and dominant, others are small anomalies. This produces a world that doesn't feel tiled or procedurally uniform.

**How mineral zones affect flora:**

**Photosynthetic flora — fronds are constant, structural tissue varies.** The photosynthetic pigment in fronds absorbs the same wavelengths regardless of soil chemistry. Fronds are dark red-violet everywhere. But the structural tissue — trunks, stalks, root-mass, the scaffolding that holds the fronds up — is built from whatever minerals are locally available. Flora can't choose which minerals to absorb; it takes what's in the groundwater and incorporates it into structural compounds. The result:

- Iron-rich zone: trunks incorporate iron oxides → warm red-brown to rust-colored structural tissue
- Copper-rich zone: trunks incorporate copper compounds → greenish to verdigris-tinted structural tissue
- Manganese-rich zone: trunks incorporate manganese oxides → very dark, near-black structural tissue
- Mixed or depleted zone: minimal mineral incorporation → pale, fibrous, more "woody" structural tissue (the default on mineral-poor substrate)

The frond canopy overhead looks the same everywhere (dark crimson). The trunks below vary. Walking through a forest, you might not notice the fronds changing (they don't), but you'd notice the trunks shifting from reddish to greenish as you cross a geological boundary. The ground mat color shifts too — the mat incorporates the same minerals as the trunks, subtly tinting the ground layer.

**Chemotrophic flora — color IS chemistry.** Already described above. Chemotrophic organisms process specific minerals and take on their color. In manganese zones: purple to violet-black. In copper zones: blue-green to turquoise. In iron zones: rusty ochre. The chemotrophic color is a direct, honest signal of what mineral is being metabolized.

**The surface-underground relationship is suggestive, not reliable.** Surface soil chemistry and deep underground mineral chemistry are shaped by different processes on different timescales. Deep minerals percolate upward through fracture networks and groundwater, but those paths are lateral as much as vertical — a copper vein at depth might enrich surface soil kilometers away, or not reach the surface at all. Surface soil is also reworked by erosion, sediment transport, volcanic ash, and organic accumulation. The mineral signature of surface soil is a mix of local bedrock weathering, transported sediment, and deep percolation — not a clean readout of what's directly below.

On a tectonically active archipelago, this decoupling is even stronger. The surface geology might be from a completely different episode than the deep mineral veins.

Near active geological features — hot springs, volcanic vents, seeps that breach the surface — the correlation is stronger. Minerals are being actively pushed up and deposited locally. Copper-green trunks near a hot spring probably do indicate copper chemistry nearby, including underground. But copper-green trunks on a hillside three kilometers away might mean copper washed in via groundwater from a completely unrelated source.

The player who treats trunk color as geological certainty will sometimes be wrong. The player who treats it as a suggestive hint — worth investigating but not proof — will be right more often. This is how real geological prospecting works.

**What the player sees:** Walking across a volcanic island, the forest canopy is uniformly dark crimson overhead. But the forest floor and the trunks change. A region of reddish-brown trunks gives way to a stretch of greenish trunks. The ground mats shift subtly in tone. The player who's paying attention notices: the soil chemistry changed. This is primarily atmospheric variety that prevents forests from feeling monotone. Near active geological features (hot springs, vents), the trunk color might also hint at what's underground — but the hint is unreliable elsewhere.

#### Flora and the Animal Clades

Flora is completely unrelated to either animal clade. The animals eat the flora, live among the flora, and shelter in the flora, but the trees are not Clade A and the fungal mats are not Clade B. Three independent lineages coexist on the planet:

The microbial-descended flora (producers — both photosynthetic and chemotrophic varieties). The two animal clades (consumers — both radiating into every animal niche). The flora provides the energy base. The animals compete with each other on top of it. The biome identity comes from which *type of flora* dominates locally (photosynthetic forest vs chemotrophic fungal zone), and both animal clades are present in both biome types.

### 6. Climate Regime

**Biome differentiation:** Chemistry-driven. Biome differences come from what minerals are in the local soil and water, not primarily from temperature or rainfall. Geological activity (highly active) is the upstream engine that produces chemical variety — but what organisms experience and respond to is the chemistry itself.

- A "forest" is a nutrient-rich wet zone where photosynthetic flora dominates — dark, low-canopied fern swamp with standing water between mineral-encrusted trunks. Both animal clades are present as consumers.
- A "fungal zone" is a manganese-rich zone where chemotrophic flora dominates — open, mounded mineral flats with bracket shelving on rock faces and fruiting spires. Both animal clades are present as consumers.
- A "desert" is a mineral-depleted zone where the geology has gone quiet — not hot and dry, but chemically dead. Neither flora type thrives. Sparse, specialized life only.
- A "wetland" is a contested zone where mineral-rich water mixes with organic runoff, supporting both photosynthetic and chemotrophic flora side by side. The highest biodiversity on the planet — both flora types and both animal clades overlap.
- "Rock/mountain" is a mineral-present but ice-possible zone — high elevation where the temperature drops enough for freezing. A niche for rare cold-adapted specialists.

Biomes are defined by which *flora type* dominates locally, which is determined by the local mineral chemistry and light availability. Both animal clades are present in every biome. The biome determines the producers; the clade determines the consumers.

**Temperature range:** Moderate (±15-20°C across the planet). Most of the world is temperate. Ice exists at elevation extremes but is uncommon and locally significant. No global ice caps. No scorching equatorial zones. Temperature is a secondary biome differentiator, not the primary one.

**Continental layout:** Archipelago. Many islands and shallow seas. Lots of coastline relative to interior land. Fragmented landmasses with channels and shallow seas between them.

Consequences:
- **Isolation drives speciation.** Islands separated by water develop different creature variants. The same ancestral predator on two different islands might look very different after enough generations. This justifies visual and behavioral variety within creature categories — and the divergence applies to both clades independently.
- **Coastline is everywhere.** The boundary between water and land is abundant. Coastal, tidal, and marsh biomes are the most common land type, not an edge case.
- **Shallow seas are highways.** For aquatic and amphibious creatures, the shallow seas between islands are easy travel routes. Surface creatures are isolated; aquatic creatures are connected. Aquatic life may be more genetically uniform (connected populations) while land life is more varied (isolated populations). This applies to both clades.
- **No deep continental interior.** An archipelago has no point on land that's far from the coast. Every terrestrial biome exists within the context of nearby water. This supports the "mostly marshy/wet" baseline.

## Summary — What the Player Sees

**Important: what the player "sees" is three layers deep.** The raw material colors of this world are very dark — the dim star produces a near-black landscape in absolute terms. The player never sees raw color. They see the creature's perceptual interpretation: a visual system evolved under this star, brightening and contrast-enhancing a dark world into something navigable. The warm brown grounds, the visible crimson trees, the amber-gold water highlights — these are post-perceptual. The "true" world in raw reflected light is much darker than what's on screen.

A dim, amber-lit world of islands and shallow seas. Soft light, mild temperatures, damp air. Three independent lineages of life — microbial-descended flora (the producers) and two unrelated animal clades (the consumers) — overlap everywhere.

The photosynthetic zones use a palette anchored in deep red — the photosynthetic pigment — but the ground is NOT the same shade as the living canopy. Forests are low-ceilinged fern swamps: crimson fronds and mineral-encrusted trunks over a near-black floor. Open grasslands are warmer and browner — a mix of living red mat, dead amber-brown detritus, and exposed mineral substrate, producing an overall warm brown tone with red accent. The visual variety comes from light exposure (shade darkens dramatically), decomposition state (dead material shifts browner), and mineral substrate (local chemistry tints the ground). The chemotrophic zones provide the strongest color contrast: open mineral flats with purple, blue-green, or rusty-ochre colony structures depending on local chemistry. These zones look distinctly different from the red-spectrum photosynthetic areas.

Both animal clades are present in every biome. Body plans are experimental and varied — familiar roles (predator, prey, grazer) but unfamiliar shapes, distinctly alien but still believable. Clade A descendants are readable: they commit to behavioral states, they have personality, they remember, they can be learned and outplayed through understanding. Clade B descendants are manageable: they run parallel behaviors, they synchronize with nearby kin, they're lethal in home territory and weaker outside it, and they're controlled through spatial awareness rather than behavioral prediction.

Underground is not a dead zone. It's a self-sustaining ecology powered by geothermal energy and mineral-rich water — chemotrophic flora and both animal clades thrive. Going deeper means entering a richer, more alien ecology — not an emptier one.

Ice and cold are alien on this planet. Creatures adapted to frozen environments are rare specialists in a world that is otherwise uniformly temperate and wet.

## Color Interpretation Guide

**The three-layer model — apply this to all visual design decisions:**

```
What the player sees = material color × environmental modification × perceptual interpretation
```

**Layer 1 — Material color (color in a vacuum):** The actual pigment or surface chemistry of the object. Photosynthetic tissue is deep red-violet. Mineral-impregnated trunk material varies by local chemistry. Dead organic material is amber-brown. Soil is mineral-tinted. This is the "true" color under neutral white light.

**Layer 2 — Environmental modification:** The yellow-orange star shifts everything warmer and darker. Shade from canopy darkens further. Moisture changes reflectance (wet surfaces are darker and slightly more reflective). Atmospheric haze at distance washes out contrast. This is physics acting on the material.

**Layer 3 — Perceptual interpretation:** The creature's visual transducers evolved under this star. They compensate for the dim light by amplifying signal — brightening the visual field. What's rendered on screen is this post-perceptual version. The raw world is much darker. The creature sees a brighter, more contrast-enhanced version of reality.

## Prompt Reference

When designing creatures, biomes, or environments, include this file so the chat knows:
- Three lineages: microbial-descended flora (producers) + two animal clades (consumers)
- Flora is NOT descended from either animal clade — it's a separate, older lineage
- Photosynthetic flora: dark, fern-like, broad/low canopy, spore-reproducing, no flowers
- Chemotrophic flora: mounded colonies, bracket shelving, fruiting spires, mineral-colored
- **Three-layer color model:** material color (pigment/chemistry in vacuum) × environmental modification (starlight, shade, moisture) × perceptual interpretation (creature's visual system brightening a dark world). What the player sees on screen is layer 3. The raw world is much darker.
- The photosynthetic anchor is deep red-violet pigment. Ground is NOT uniform red — it's a mix of living mat, dead detritus, and mineral substrate producing warm brown tones with red accent. Trees/fronds are the most visibly red element.
- Chemotrophic zones provide the strongest color contrast (purple, blue-green, ochre depending on mineral chemistry)
- Regional mineral zones create visual variation: photosynthetic fronds are constant dark red everywhere, but trunks vary by local surface soil mineral incorporation (iron-red, copper-green, manganese-dark). Surface chemistry loosely correlates with underground chemistry near active geological features, but the relationship is not reliable.
- What colors are physically justified (dark photosynthetic life, mineral-tinted chemotrophic life, metallic vs calcium animal structures)
- Why biomes differ (which flora type dominates, driven by mineral chemistry)
- Why underground is rich (geothermal + chemotrophic flora + both animal clades)
- Why animal body plans are weird (Cambrian-level, two clades radiating into all niches)
- Why creatures in the same niche can feel very different (different clade ancestry)
- What the two AI paradigms are (Clade A: state machine, readable. Clade B: parallel, spatial)
- Why Clade B groups are more dangerous than Clade B individuals (ganglion synchronization)
- Why islands have different creature variants (archipelago isolation, applies to both clades)
- What "desert" means on this planet (mineral depletion, not heat)
- What ice means on this planet (rare, alien, high-elevation specialist niche)
