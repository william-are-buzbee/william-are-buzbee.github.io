# Underground Chemotrophic Ecology

Addition to Ecology-Foundations.md. Insert after the Flora section (after "Flora and the Animal Clades") and before "6. Climate Regime." This section defines the underground environment, its energy source, its flora, and the animal niches it supports. Everything downstream — underground creature design, biome construction, the colonial chemotroph body map — is constrained by what's established here.

## The Underground Energy System

### The Energy Source — Redox Chemistry

The planet's interior is hot and geologically active. It continuously pushes **reduced compounds** upward through the crust — metal sulfides, reduced iron, reduced manganese, dissolved hydrogen, and other mineral species that have not yet reacted with oxygen. These compounds are chemically loaded. When they encounter oxidized conditions (oxygen-containing air or water percolating down from the surface), the reaction releases energy. That reaction — reduced compounds meeting an oxidant — is the energy source that powers all underground life.

The critical structure is the **redox gradient**: the boundary layer between reduced conditions below (hot, mineral-laden, oxygen-poor) and oxidized conditions above (cooler, oxygen-present from surface atmosphere). Life concentrates at and around this boundary. The gradient is not a flat horizontal line — it follows geology. Wherever fractured rock lets mineral-rich fluid rise, wherever water percolates downward to meet rising compounds, the gradient steepens locally and energy density spikes. These spikes are the productive sites. The underground ecology is a network of productive sites connected by lower-energy passages.

**The depth-energy relationship:** Deeper means closer to the source. Shallow underground environments receive minerals that have already partially reacted during their long percolation upward — the energy has been partially spent. Deep underground environments near active geological features (fault lines, magma-proximate chambers, geothermal vents) receive fresh, fully reduced compounds directly. The deepest accessible environments are the most energy-dense on the planet — potentially more productive than the dim photosynthetic surface, because the sun is weak but the planet's core is hot.

### Oxygen Production Underground

On Earth, chemotrophic organisms typically *consume* oxygen as their electron acceptor — they need O₂ to oxidize their mineral substrates. Underground ecosystems on Earth that support animal life are borrowing oxygen that percolated down from the photosynthetic surface. They are subsidized, not self-sustaining. Animal complexity is limited by oxygen supply, which thins with depth.

This planet is different. The dominant underground chemotrophic flora lineage evolved a metabolic pathway that **splits water as part of its mineral-processing chemistry**, releasing O₂ as a metabolic byproduct. The energy from oxidizing reduced minerals drives the water-splitting reaction. This is biochemically analogous to how cyanobacteria on Earth use light energy to split water during photosynthesis — but here, chemical energy provides the driving force instead of photons.

The consequence is straightforward: **wherever chemotrophic flora grows densely, it produces breathable oxygen.** Oxygen availability underground tracks flora density, which tracks mineral substrate availability. Rich mineral zones support thick flora, which produces abundant O₂, which supports complex animal life. Depleted zones support thin or absent flora, which produces little or no O₂, which suffocates animals.

This makes the underground ecology genuinely self-sustaining. It does not depend on oxygen diffusing down from the surface. Deep environments with rich mineral substrate and dense flora have better air than shallow environments with depleted substrate and sparse flora. The oxygen gradient can run opposite to what intuition suggests — deeper can mean more breathable, not less, if the geology is active.

**What the player experiences:** Lush chemotrophic growth means breathable air. Thin mats on bare rock mean thinning air. Barren rock with no visible flora means dead air — the player is on a clock. The biology provides a readable signal: flora density = air quality. The player learns this through experience and it becomes a survival skill.

## Underground Environment Zones

The underground is not one biome. It is a network of microenvironments defined by four variables: **mineral energy density, water availability, oxygen concentration, and physical space.** These combine into distinct zone types that the player experiences as fundamentally different habitats.

### Seep Zones

Where mineral-rich fluid actively emerges from deeper rock — fissures, hot springs, upwelling pools. The highest energy density available at any given depth. Flora grows thick here, concentrated around the fluid source: colony mounds clustered near the upwelling, bracket shelving on surrounding walls, mat networks spreading across every wet surface, flow-adapted streamers in any running water. Oxygen production is high.

The air is warm, humid, breathable, faintly metallic. The water is warm and saturated with dissolved minerals. Surfaces near the seep are encrusted with mineral deposits — the geological equivalent of fertile soil.

**Seep zones are the oases.** Everything downstream depends on them. They are the anchors of underground ecology, the sites around which animal territories are built, and the destinations that migration routes connect.

### Flow Zones

Streams and channels carrying mineral-rich water away from seeps through the tunnel network. Flora lines the waterways, processing dissolved minerals as the water flows past. Flow-adapted flora (streamers and branching filamentous structures) is the dominant growth form here. Mat flora carpets the wet rock alongside the channels.

Productivity tracks water supply. A well-fed channel carrying rich mineral water supports dense growth and good oxygen. A dying trickle from a depleted or distant seep supports thin mats and stale air.

**Flow zones are the highways.** They connect oases. Animals follow them because the water carries the energy supply chain. The presence of a flow zone tells the player there is a productive source upstream and possibly another downstream.

### Substrate Zones

Exposed rock faces with reduced minerals in the stone but no active water delivery. Flora grows here as slow-spreading mats and bracket shelves, processing the rock itself grain by grain. Growth is slow. Energy output is low. Oxygen production is thin but present.

**Substrate zones are the scrubland.** Sustainable but sparse. Animals that live here are adapted to low productivity — slow metabolisms, efficient extraction, small or slow bodies. A Clade A animal might pass through on its way between richer zones. A Clade B animal might establish permanent territory if the substrate is steady enough.

### Dead Zones

Already-oxidized rock. The minerals have been processed — either by flora over long timescales or by geological exposure. Nothing left to metabolize. No flora grows here. No local oxygen production. Whatever air is present drifted in from adjacent zones and is getting staler.

**Dead zones are the deserts.** Animals cross them but cannot stay. The longer the tunnel between productive zones, the more dangerous the crossing. Transit through a dead zone requires metabolic reserves and oxygen tolerance. Knowing where the next productive zone is before entering a dead zone is a survival skill — for animals and for the player.

Dead zones gate movement through the underground. They create natural barriers between productive pockets, isolating populations the same way ocean channels isolate island populations on the surface. Two seep complexes separated by a long dead zone may have divergent species — same ancestral stock, independently evolving in chemical isolation.

### Deep Zones

Near active geological features — fault lines, magma-proximate chambers, the boundary where the planet's interior is actively resurfacing reduced compounds. Extreme mineral richness. Extreme heat. Potentially elevated concentrations of toxic volcanic gases alongside high oxygen from hyper-productive flora.

**Deep zones are the richest ecosystems on the planet.** More energy-dense than the photosynthetic surface. Flora grows in forms not seen at shallower depths — larger colony mounds, denser mat coverage, novel growth forms adapted to extreme heat and chemistry. Animal life is abundant, diverse, and adapted to conditions that would kill surface organisms.

The deep zones are also the most physically hostile. Heat stress, caustic water chemistry, unstable geology. The energy is there but accessing it requires deep adaptation. This creates a natural progression: the player encounters shallow, sparse underground first, then gradually discovers that depth means richness, not emptiness — but the price of that richness is an increasingly alien and demanding environment.

## Underground Flora — Growth Forms

The underground chemotrophic flora is the same lineage described in the Flora section above — descended from chemotrophic microbial mats, scaled up into colonial macro-organisms. On the surface, this lineage competes with photosynthetic flora for space and is limited to areas where mineral chemistry outcompetes light availability. Underground, freed from that competition, the chemotrophic lineage has diversified into the full range of producer strategies.

All growth forms below are the same lineage in different configurations responding to different physical conditions. A rich seep zone may contain all of them simultaneously. A marginal substrate zone may support only mats.

### Mat Flora

Continuous films and thin mats coating wet rock surfaces, processing whatever mineral substrate they sit on. The baseline growth form — the simplest, most ubiquitous, first to colonize new substrate. Mats appear anywhere there is mineral substrate and moisture. They are the "grass" of the underground.

Visual character: thin, flat, slightly glossy. Color matches local mineral chemistry (copper-green, iron-rust, manganese-violet). Texture ranges from smooth biofilm on wet rock to slightly crusty on drier surfaces. Easy to overlook — the player may not immediately recognize that the discolored rock surface is alive.

### Colony Mounds

The large-bodied producer. Domed, layered structures built up in concentric mineral-encrusted layers over long timescales. Concentrated around seeps and rich substrate where energy density supports sustained growth. These are the "trees" of the underground — the dominant structural organisms of productive zones. A mature seep zone is a cluster of colony mounds with mat flora carpeting everything between them.

Colony mounds are the same structures described in the surface chemotrophic flora section but larger and more elaborate underground, where they are unconstrained by surface weathering and have access to richer mineral substrate. Deep-zone colony mounds may be significantly larger than anything on the surface.

### Flow-Adapted Flora (Streamers)

Branching filamentous structures anchored to rock in flowing water, maximizing surface area in the current. They process dissolved minerals from the water as it flows past. Found in every flow zone. The "river weeds" of the underground.

Dense enough in well-fed channels to partially obstruct narrow passages, creating a physical navigation hazard. The player may need to push through or cut through streamer growth to follow a waterway. In a rich flow zone, the streamers themselves are a significant biomass — a food source for grazers that can access them.

### Bracket Flora

Horizontal shelf structures projecting from walls and ceilings. The same form as surface bracket shelving but more elaborate underground where vertical rock surfaces are abundant and the flora has no competitors. In large caverns, bracket flora can form tiered shelving up the walls — each shelf a flat colonial organism processing the rock face it's attached to.

Bracket shelves are stable, long-lived structures. They indicate established, stable environments — the presence of large bracket growth tells the player this area has been productive for a long time.

### Spire Flora

Thin vertical structures for spore dispersal. The same form as surface fruiting spires. Underground, where air currents are confined and directional (following tunnel geometry, driven by temperature differentials between zones), the spires orient toward drafts. Their placement and lean maps airflow patterns — a biological indicator of tunnel ventilation that an observant player could read.

Spires are reproductive organs of the colony organisms, not independent entities. Their presence indicates a mature, reproductively active colony.

## Underground Animal Niches

Given the environment defined above, these are the ecological jobs available to animals underground. This is a niche catalog, not a creature list — any niche could be filled by a Clade A descendant, a Clade B descendant, or both independently.

### Grazer

Eats flora directly. Bites, scrapes, or pulls apart mat, colony mound, bracket shelf, or streamer tissue. Digests the mineral-encrusted organic material.

**The key adaptation is metal tolerance.** Chemotrophic flora incorporates heavy metals and mineral compounds into its tissue — that is what it processes. An animal that eats this flora ingests high concentrations of copper, iron, manganese, or whatever the local substrate chemistry produces. Surviving on this diet requires evolved metal tolerance: sequestration (binding metals into inert storage compounds in specific tissues), excretion (efficient elimination of excess metals through specialized organs), and potentially metal *use* (incorporating metals into structural compounds, pigments, or enzymatic processes).

**The consequence for the animal's body:** An underground grazer's tissue chemistry reflects its diet. Its structural compounds may incorporate metals. Its pigmentation comes from metal byproducts. Its armor or integument, if any, may be metal-hardened or metal-tinted. The deeper the grazer lives — where flora processes richer substrate — the higher the metal concentration in the food chain, and the more metal-saturated the grazer's own tissue becomes.

**The consequence for the player:** When the player kills and consumes an underground grazer, the tissue is chemically different from surface animal tissue. The metal content may drive different mutation pressures than surface meat. Deeper grazers are more metal-saturated, which could mean stronger or more unusual mutation effects. The food chain carries the mineral signature of the environment all the way up to whoever eats at the top.

This is the most abundant underground niche. Wherever flora grows, something eats it. Size range is wide — small grazers cropping mat flora, larger grazers that can break into colony mounds.

### Absorber

Does not eat solid flora. Sits in mineral-rich water — at seeps, in pools, in slow-moving flow zones — and hosts **symbiotic chemotrophic microbes** in or on its body. The symbionts process dissolved minerals through the host's permeable integument and share metabolic products with the host's cells.

**How this works biologically:** The animal's integument (skin, outer tissue layer) has evolved to be porous or semi-permeable in specific body regions. Chemotrophic microbes — descendants of the same ancestral microbial lineage that produced the flora — colonize the integument surface and the porous tissue layers beneath it. When the animal is immersed in or resting on mineral-rich substrate, dissolved minerals pass through the integument and reach the microbial colony. The microbes metabolize the minerals (the same chemistry the free-living flora performs) and release organic metabolic products that the host animal's cells absorb as nutrition.

**Feeding is passive and positional.** The animal does not hunt, chase, or chew. It finds a productive site — a mineral-rich pool, a seep margin, a patch of fresh substrate — and occupies it. Metabolic intake occurs continuously while the animal is positioned on productive substrate. The behavior looks like basking or resting. It is actually grazing at a microbial scale.

**The animal's surface is an active chemical process.** The symbiont colony's metabolic activity produces enzymatic byproducts on the host's outer integument — the same caustic secretions that free-living chemotrophic mats produce when processing minerals. These secretions do not distinguish between mineral substrate and organic matter at the chemical level. Contact with the animal's active integument is contact with an ongoing chemical reaction. The creature is not attacking — it is simply chemically active on its surface at all times while feeding.

**What makes this different from grazing:** Grazers exploit solid flora tissue. Absorbers exploit dissolved minerals that grazers cannot access — you cannot bite dissolved iron out of water, but symbionts on your integument can process it. The two niches are complementary, not competitive. Both can coexist at the same seep: grazers eating the colony mounds while absorbers sit in the mineral-rich pools between them.

### Scavenger

Eats dead organic matter — dead animals, dead flora tissue, organic debris accumulating in low-flow areas and pool margins. Tolerant of low-oxygen conditions because dead material collects in stagnant zones where flora is sparse and air is thin. The underground equivalent of a detritivore.

Scavengers can survive in marginal environments where neither grazing nor absorbing provides sufficient energy alone. They fill the gaps — the edges of dead zones, the stagnant backwaters, the depleted substrate areas where productive life is thin but organic debris still accumulates from more productive zones upstream.

### Predator

Eats other animals. The same trophic role as surface predators, but adapted to the underground's sensory environment.

**Underground sensory conditions reshape predation.** Confined spaces amplify vibration — every footstep propagates through rock and is detectable at significant range. Chemical signals persist longer in still, humid underground air than in open surface air with wind dispersal. Visual range is limited by darkness, tunnel geometry, and the absence of ambient light (except bioluminescence from flora or other organisms, if any).

Underground predation favors **ambush and territory knowledge** over pursuit. The terrain is confined. Pursuit requires knowing the tunnel network — dead ends are lethal for both predator and prey. A predator that knows the local tunnel geometry has a massive advantage: it knows which passages connect, where prey is cornered, and where escape routes exist. An unfamiliar predator in an unfamiliar tunnel system is far less effective.

### Sessile Filter

Anchored in flowing water, straining organic particles and dissolved compounds from the current. The animal equivalent of the flow-adapted flora — positioned in the same habitat, exploiting similar resources, but consuming organic particulates (dead cells, detritus, microbes) rather than processing inorganic minerals.

Possible at any scale, from small encrusting animals on flow-zone walls to larger reef-like structures that partially block tunnels. Visually, sessile filters may be difficult for the player to distinguish from flora — both are immobile, both are encrusted with mineral deposits, both grow on rock surfaces in flowing water. The distinction matters when the player touches or harvests what they thought was a plant and it reacts.

### Oasis Specialist

Lives exclusively in and around a single seep zone. Does not cross dead zones. Does not migrate. The entire life cycle — feeding, reproduction, territory, death — happens within one productive pocket.

Extremely adapted to local conditions: metal tolerance matched to the specific mineral chemistry of that seep. May be genetically distinct from the same species at a different seep because the populations are isolated by dead zones between them. The archipelago speciation pattern from the surface, repeated underground at smaller scale. Two colonies of the same ancestral species at two different seeps, separated by a dead zone, may have diverged enough to be functionally different organisms with different mineral tolerances, different coloration, and different tissue chemistry.

### Migration Specialist

Crosses dead zones between productive areas. Carries enough metabolic reserve and oxygen tolerance to survive transit through depleted tunnels. Knows the network — has learned or inherited knowledge of where productive zones are and how to reach them.

This is intrinsically a high-cognition niche. Crossing dead zones without a map is suicide — the animal needs to know (or sense at range) that a productive zone exists at the other end before committing to the crossing. A wrong turn in a dead zone means running out of air.

## How the Clades Respond to the Underground

Both clades colonized underground environments independently. The ecology doc already establishes that both clades are present in every biome. But the clade architectures create natural affinities for different underground strategies.

### Clade A Underground

Clade A's cognitive architecture — centralized brain, episodic memory, chemical-dominant sensing — maps onto underground life with specific strengths.

**Chemical sensing is more effective underground.** In confined spaces with limited airflow, chemical signals persist longer and disperse more slowly than on the surface. A chemical trail in a tunnel is readable for far longer than a chemical trail in open air with wind dispersal. A Clade A predator tracking prey by scent through a tunnel network has a stronger signal than the same predator on the surface. The primary sense gets *better*, not worse.

**Episodic memory maps the tunnel network.** A Clade A animal that has explored a tunnel system remembers it — which passages connect to seeps, which are dead ends, where the dead zones are and how long the crossings take. Older individuals have more complete maps. This makes experienced Clade A animals disproportionately more dangerous underground than on the surface, because terrain knowledge matters more in confined, branching spaces than in open terrain.

**Migration is a Clade A specialty.** Crossing dead zones requires spatial planning and experience accumulation — remembering where the productive zones are, estimating whether metabolic reserves are sufficient for the crossing, learning from failed or near-failed attempts. This is centralized cognition work. A Clade A grazer that migrates between seep zones on a learned circuit, timing arrivals to when flora has regrown since the last visit, is using the same cognitive infrastructure as a surface Clade A predator planning a hunt — just applied to resource management instead of predation.

**Clade A underground creatures are mobile, planful, and dangerous because they know where you are going.**

### Clade B Underground

Clade B's architecture — distributed ganglia, vibration-dominant sensing, territory-trained pattern libraries — is not just viable underground. It is arguably *better suited* to underground life than surface life.

**Vibration sensing is superior underground.** Rock transmits vibration further and more clearly than loose soil or surface substrate. Every footstep, every movement, every disturbance propagates through tunnel walls and floors with high fidelity. A Clade B organism in a familiar tunnel system knows every vibration signature of every surface — the resonance of each passage, the damping characteristics of each rock type, the normal background vibration from water flow and geological settling. A novel vibration (the player walking, fighting, mining) is unmistakable against this trained baseline. Underground Clade B vibration sensing is effectively sonar mapped against memorized terrain.

**Territory mastery intensifies.** On the surface, a Clade B animal's territory is a patch of ground it knows well. Underground, a territory is a three-dimensional tunnel network — walls, floor, ceiling, branching passages, resonance characteristics. The pattern library each ganglion builds is richer because the environment is more structured. Familiar-territory advantage is even larger underground than on the surface.

**Colonial architecture flourishes.** A Clade B colony can spread across a seep zone the same way the flora does, with individual nodes positioned to maximize coverage of the productive area. Underground colonial organisms have a structural advantage: the tunnel geometry constrains approach routes. A colony doesn't need to watch in all directions — it needs to watch the passages that enter its territory. Nodes positioned at tunnel junctions and chokepoints create a detection and defense network shaped by the physical space.

**The absorber niche is a natural fit for colonial Clade B.** A colonial organism with nodes sitting in mineral-rich water, hosting symbiotic chemotrophic microbes, collectively managing a seep or pool — this is the colonial chemotroph (Creature 5) as currently designed, but grounded in the environment rather than described in isolation. The nodes are not a "swarm." They are a distributed colonial animal managing a mineral territory, each node processing dissolved minerals through its symbiont-colonized integument, the colony collectively covering the productive area the way the flora covers substrate.

**Sessile and semi-sessile strategies are available.** Clade B's flexible integument and distributed neural architecture support a gradient from fully mobile to fully anchored. A Clade B sessile filter in a flow zone is an animal that gradually reduced its mobility as it committed to a fixed position in productive flowing water — its ganglia still process vibration, still run pattern libraries, still coordinate with nearby individuals if they're close enough to synchronize. But it doesn't walk anymore. It doesn't need to.

**Clade B underground creatures are territorial, embedded, and dangerous because you walked into where they are.**

### The Player's Experience of Underground Ecology

The same dynamic as the surface, intensified. Clade A creatures are encountered on the move — traveling between zones, following learned routes, tracking the player by chemical trail through passages. The player deals with them through evasion, misdirection, and exploiting the fact that Clade A commits to a behavioral state and can be read during transitions.

Clade B creatures are encountered in place — occupying territories, anchored to productive sites, detecting the player through vibration as soon as the player enters their tunnel system. The player deals with them through spatial awareness: knowing which zones are occupied, avoiding territories, reducing vibration (moving slowly, choosing substrate carefully), and not allowing colonial synchronization to build up by entering too deep into a colony's territory.

Underground, both paradigms are heightened. Clade A chemical tracking is stronger (persistent signals in still air). Clade B vibration detection is stronger (high-fidelity signal transmission through rock). The confined spaces make encounters harder to avoid and harder to disengage from. Depth increases both the richness of the ecology and the intensity of the threats — more creatures, better adapted, in an environment that favors them over the player.

## The Depth-Energy Gradient — What the Player Experiences

The underground is not uniformly hostile or uniformly rich. The player experiences a gradient that inverts the surface assumption of "underground = scarcity."

**Shallow underground:** Sparse substrate zones and scattered small seeps. Thin mat flora on rock walls. Air is present but not abundant — the player notices it thinning occasionally. Animal life is sparse, small, and adapted to low productivity. Threats are minor. Resources are limited. This is the accessible introduction to underground ecology.

**Mid-depth:** Larger seep complexes connected by flow-zone networks. Colony mounds appear. Bracket shelving covers walls in productive areas. Animal diversity increases — grazers, predators, absorbers, the beginning of colonial territories. Dead zones between productive pockets create navigation challenges. The player must learn to read flora density as an air-quality indicator and plan routes between productive zones. Threats are moderate and the ecology is visibly richer than the surface chemotrophic zones.

**Deep:** Massive geothermal productivity. Large colony mounds, dense mat coverage, thick streamer growth in waterways, tiered bracket shelving. Oxygen is abundant in productive zones. Animal life is diverse and well-adapted — underground specialists that have never seen the surface. Metal saturation in the food chain is high: animal tissue is mineral-dense, coloration is intense, structural compounds are metal-hardened. Threats are serious. The environment itself is physically demanding: heat, caustic water, unstable geology. But the energy available is the highest on the planet.

**The inversion:** The player enters the underground expecting it to get worse. Instead, it gets *richer* — more life, more resources, more oxygen, more complexity. But also more dangerous, more alien, and more demanding. The deepest environments are simultaneously the most productive ecosystems on the planet and the most hostile physical environments. The tension between abundance and danger is the core experience of deep underground exploration.

## What This Constrains Downstream

| Decision | Constrained by |
|---|---|
| Underground air quality | Flora density, which tracks mineral substrate availability |
| Underground biome identity | Zone type (seep, flow, substrate, dead, deep) defined by mineral and water availability |
| Underground creature viability | Oxygen production by chemotrophic flora — no flora means no air means no animals |
| Underground creature diets | Metal-rich flora tissue, dissolved minerals, organic detritus — all carry mineral chemistry signatures |
| Underground creature tissue chemistry | Diet-driven metal accumulation — deeper creatures have more metal-saturated tissue |
| Player mutation from underground food | Different metal content in underground animal tissue vs surface animal tissue |
| Underground Clade A behavior | Enhanced chemical tracking (still air) + tunnel network memory (episodic spatial mapping) |
| Underground Clade B behavior | Enhanced vibration detection (rock transmission) + intensified territory mastery (structured 3D space) |
| Colonial chemotroph biology | Absorber niche — symbiont-hosted, passive mineral processing, enzymatic surface is metabolic byproduct not weapon |
| Dead zone function | Navigation gates, population isolators, oxygen barriers — the underground equivalent of ocean channels |
| Depth = richness, not scarcity | Active geology delivers richer substrate at depth, supporting more flora, more oxygen, more life |
| Underground society viability (future) | Deep geothermal zones provide more energy than the surface — civilization is possible where energy is highest |
